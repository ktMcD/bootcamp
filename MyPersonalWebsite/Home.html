Radio

Turntables, records and jackets (oh my!)

Many of us arrive at our careers by chance. I actively pursued a career in development. My life in broadcasting, however, was a different matter. I attended a private religious high school in Ashland, Oregon, at which I was able to earn most of my tuition in exchange for working at their radio station. After leaving the Marine Corps, I was somewhat at sixes and sevens as I acclimated to civilian life. On a lark, I called a local radio station in Klamath Falls, Oregon, to see if they had an opening. They hired me on the spot for the evening music shift. Luck. Timing.

Today, after 36 years, I'm once again on Sunny 107. It's been such a pleasure to be back on the air at my one of my old stomping grounds. I've enjoyed being back on the air so much that I'm now pursuing other contract voice over and voice track opportunities.

Wheels

There's a reason for rain gear

The most important contributor to my enjoyment of any hobby is gear. It's all all about the gear. After a lifetime of living in the Pacific Northwest, I know this: I will get wet. When I'm on my bike, I know that dark cloud ahead has my name on it. Gear, baby. Lots of it. Bags. Rain gear. Gloves. Heated clothing. Best helmet available in my budget. It all adds up to ensure that I'm not riding distracted by physical discomfort so I can focus on staying out of the way and out of the hospital.

These boots are better be made for walking [sorry, Nancy]

New riders choose their boots for different reasons: style, cool factor, safety. It's not enough to think only about safety when selecting a riding boot. It's not just about the journey. It's also about exploring those little nooks and crannies along the way. If those boots are safe or cool or sexy and painful? Well then, it's just no fun. It's as easy to find good boots that are cool and comfortable as it is to grab a brand name that doesn't fill the whole bill. I keep hoping that Uggs will start making riding boots.

Sticks & String

The closest I will ever get to magic is to make a thing out of another thing.

Let's talk a little more about gear. We tend to scrimp when we buy our first setup for any new hobby - whether it's a cheap guitar only playable by someone who can palm a basketball, a scrappy used rat bike to learn on or grocery store knitting needles and bargain yarn. This approach isn't conducive to real enjoyment; it may cause so much frustration that you give up before you master the learning curve. You'll have enough to contend with when you're first learning. You don't need to fight with your gear, too.

Buy good yarn. Buy good needles. Buy the best you can afford. Don't miss out on the pleasures of your new hobby by shortchanging yourself on supplies.
